Contracts Between Services

How we approach integration and contract testing across the platform.

When two services talk, each carries its own assumptions about what the other promises. When those assumptions drift apart, things break in production even though every test passed — because the failure lives in the gap between the services, which no single service's tests exercise.

In brief

  • Integration testing checks a service against its contract from the outside — through its interface, not its internals.
  • Verify each boundary against a single shared contract rather than standing up every service together.
  • The contract is one shared artifact both sides bind to, not two private copies that can drift.
  • Who owns the contract depends on the boundary: consumer-driven, negotiated, or provider-driven.
  • The check runs as a pre-merge gate and on demand — not on every commit or on a schedule; people own policy and approve changes, not test runs.
  • Contracts are about meaning as much as shape — identical shapes can still break a promise.

1Unit and integration tests, by what they know

The distinction worth keeping isn't size — it's what a test is allowed to know about the thing it tests.

A unit test is white-box: it knows the internals. That makes it precise, and it also means it breaks under refactoring even when behaviour hasn't changed.

An integration test is black-box: it knows only the contract — the interface. It can't see how the work is done, so it survives refactoring and fails only when observable behaviour changes. A useful side effect: black-box tests document what a service actually promises.

The boundary is relative to ownership. Everything inside a service you own is white-box to you; the test that touches only its public interface is the black-box, integration test.

2Don't stand up the whole system

The obvious approach — run every service together and check they cooperate — doesn't scale. Interaction combinations grow faster than tests can cover them, the environment is slow and flaky, and a failure tells you something in the chain broke, not where.

Decompose instead. Verify each service against its contract independently. If A satisfies the contract and B was built against the same contract, they compose — you don't need them running side by side to know it. Reserve full end-to-end runs for a few high-value paths, treated as a canary rather than as coverage.

3The contract is the shared artifact

Drift happens when each side keeps its own copy of the contract. The provider returns user_id; the consumer expects userId. Both test suites pass; production fails — because each side validated against its own version of the truth.

The remedy is a single shared contract both sides bind to. A well-formed contract package has two faces built from one source: a conformance suite that checks the provider against the contract, and a mock the consumer builds against. Neither side can drift without something turning red.

4Who owns the contract

This decision drives the rest, and it reduces to a single question: who has final say over what the contract says? There are three answers — a spectrum, not a binary.

KindWhat it isWho owns the contract
Consumer-driven
(a BFF)		 A backend built to serve one frontend — it exists for that consumer. The consumer. Its needs are the spec; the provider is verified against them.
Negotiated
(peers)		 Two co-equal services, usually different teams, neither serving the other. Neither alone. Jointly owned; changed only by mutual agreement, like a treaty.
Provider-driven
(a data product)		 A stable interface that many teams depend on. The provider. It publishes the interface; consumers conform.

The quick test is to ask who has final authority over a change. The consumer → consumer-driven. The provider → provider-driven. Both, by mutual agreement → negotiated.

Negotiated is not a default for boundaries no one has thought about. If you can't articulate why neither side is subordinate, the boundary isn't negotiated — it's unassigned, and it needs an owner. Negotiated means a deliberate treaty between equals, and it tends to be transitional: over time, one side usually becomes the natural owner.

5Two kinds of independence

Two things tend to get conflated. Keep them apart.

Authorship — who decides what the contract says (section 4).

Execution — who runs the check and reports the result. A neutral runner matters when two teams sit on either side, so neither can quietly weaken the gate. When a single team is checking its own work, it's unnecessary.

6Automate the gate; reserve people for judgment

The contract check runs in CI as a blocking step — that's what actually keeps drift out, because a check you have to remember to run gets skipped under pressure. The human role is narrower and more valuable: someone owns the compatibility policy (additive changes are fine; breaking changes need a new version) and arbitrates change requests. Automating enforcement is precisely what lets the gate stay strict without becoming a bottleneck.

When they run. These tests are heavier than unit tests — they exercise real services — so run them where they earn their cost: on demand while working on a contract change, and as a required check on the pull request that would land it. They are not a per-commit or scheduled job; running them continuously adds load and noise without catching anything a pre-merge gate wouldn't.

7Meaning, not just shape

Shape asks whether a field is a number. Meaning asks whether amount still denotes gross rather than net. The shape can be identical while the promise is broken. We verify meaning in layers, scaled to what each is worth: types and ranges; invariants (total equals the sum of line items); properties (adding an item never lowers the total); and a small, human-reviewed remainder for the meaning that resists encoding. We deliberately stop short of encoding everything — past a point, specification costs more than the defects it prevents.

8What this means for our codebase

Consumer-driven · BFF

mt-landvault-api serves the Discover Pod and nothing else, which makes it a BFF. The Discover Pod (in landvault-shell) owns the contract; we verify the API against what the Pod requires. The shell, which already integrates the pods, runs the check.

Provider-driven · Data product

The mt-data-* services (biology, chemistry, …) publish stable interfaces that many consumers depend on — data products. Each owns its contract; consumers conform; the Platform Steward governs.

Negotiated · Peers

Any boundary between two co-equal services owned by different teams, where neither serves the other: jointly owned, changed only by agreement.

9Glossary

Contract
The agreed interface between two services — what each request accepts and returns, and what that means.
BFF (Backend-for-Frontend)
A backend built to serve one specific frontend.
Data product
A service that publishes a stable interface for many independent consumers.
Conformance suite
Tests that check a running service against its contract.
Drift
When the two sides' models of the contract silently diverge.
Steward
The role that owns a contract's compatibility policy and approves changes to it.
Black-box / white-box
Testing against the public contract only, versus testing with knowledge of the internals.
