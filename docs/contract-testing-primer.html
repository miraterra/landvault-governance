Contracts Between Services

How we think about integration testing at LandVault — without the jargon.

When two services talk to each other, each one carries its own idea of what the other promises. When those ideas quietly drift apart, things break in production even though every test passed — because the bug lives in the gap between the services, where no single service's tests are looking.

We hit a real example in our own code: one endpoint that exists at three different paths — /field, /areal/predictions, and /areal/prediction — all shipping under the same version number. The route the code serves, the path the docs advertise, and the path the live server answers on had all drifted apart. No unit test catches that. This primer explains how we close that gap.

The whole idea in six lines

  • Integration testing = checking a service against its contract, through its front door — not poking its insides.
  • Don't boot all the services together. Test each boundary against one shared contract.
  • The contract is a real, shared artifact both sides bind to — not two private copies that can drift.
  • Who owns the contract depends on the boundary: consumer-driven, negotiated, or provider-driven.
  • The check is automated and blocking. People set policy and approve changes; they don't run tests by hand.
  • Test meaning, not just shape. Same shape can still be a broken promise.

1Unit vs. integration: the distinction that actually helps

Forget "small test vs. big test." The useful line is what the test is allowed to know:

A unit test is white-box — it knows the internals. That makes it precise, but it also means it breaks when you refactor, even if behavior didn't change.

An integration test is black-box — it only knows the contract (the public front door). It can't see how the work is done, so it survives refactoring and breaks only when the observable behavior changes. A nice bonus: black-box tests double as living documentation of what a service actually promises.

2Don't boot the whole world

The tempting approach is to spin up every service together and see if they talk. Don't. It doesn't scale: the number of interactions grows faster than you can write tests, the setup is slow and flaky, and when it goes red it tells you "something in the chain broke" — not where.

Instead, decompose: test each service against its contract, on its own. If service A satisfies the contract, and service B was built against the same contract, they fit together — you don't need to run them side-by-side to prove it. Keep full end-to-end runs for a small handful of critical "smoke" journeys, treated as a canary, not as your main coverage.

3The contract is the shared thing

Drift happens when each side keeps its own private copy of the contract in its head. Provider returns user_id; consumer expects userId. Both teams' tests pass. Production breaks. Nothing failed because each side tested against its own idea of the truth.

The fix is one shared contract artifact both sides bind to. A good contract package has two faces, built from one source:

① a conformance suite that checks the provider actually meets the contract, and ② a mock the consumer develops against — so neither side can drift without something going red.

4Who owns the contract? Where final authority sits

This is the decision everything else follows from. It comes down to one question: who has final say over what the contract says? There are three answers — a spectrum, not a binary.

KindWhat it isWho owns the contract
Consumer-driven
(a BFF)		 A backend built to serve one frontend — it exists for that consumer. The consumer. Its needs are the spec; the provider is verified against them.
Negotiated
(peers)		 Two co-equal services, usually different teams, neither serving the other. Neither alone. Jointly owned; changed only by mutual agreement — like a treaty.
Provider-driven
(a data product)		 A stable interface that many teams depend on. The provider. It publishes the interface; consumers conform.

Quick test: who has final say over a change? The consumer → consumer-driven. The provider → provider-driven. Both must agree → negotiated.

"Negotiated" is not a parking spot. If you can't say why neither side is in charge, the boundary isn't negotiated — it's unassigned, and you need to assign it. Negotiated means a deliberate, shared treaty between equals, not "nobody decided yet."

5Two different kinds of "independent"

These get confused, so keep them separate:

Authorship — who decides what the contract says. (That's section 4 above.)

Execution — who runs the check and reports pass/fail. A neutral runner matters when two different teams are on the line, so neither can quietly weaken the gate. When one team is just checking itself, a neutral runner is overkill.

6Automate the gate; let people do judgment

The contract check runs automatically in CI as a blocking step — that's what actually keeps drift out (a check you have to remember to run gets skipped under pressure). The human role is different and smaller: someone owns the compatibility policy ("additive changes are fine; breaking changes need a new version") and arbitrates change requests. They don't run tests by hand. Automating enforcement is exactly what lets the gate be strict without becoming a bottleneck.

7Test meaning, not just shape

Shape is "is this field a number?" Meaning is "does amount still mean gross, not net?" The shape can be identical while the promise is broken. We check meaning in layers, by how much it's worth:

① types & ranges (cheap, always) → ② invariants (total == sum of line items) → ③ properties ("adding an item never lowers the total") → ④ a small human-reviewed remainder for meaning that simply can't be encoded. We deliberately don't try to encode everything — past a point, specifying costs more than the bugs would.

8What this means for our codebase

Consumer-driven · BFF

mt-landvault-api serves the Discover Pod and nothing else. So the Discover Pod (in landvault-shell) owns the contract; we verify the API against what the Pod needs (consumer-driven, TypeScript/Pact-style). The shell — as the thing that already integrates the pods — runs the check.

Provider-driven · Data product

The mt-data-* products (biology, chemistry, …) publish stable interfaces many things depend on. They own their contracts; consumers conform; the Platform Steward governs. (The conformance-suite pattern fits these.)

Negotiated · Peers

Any boundary between two co-equal services owned by different teams, where neither is serving the other — jointly owned, changed only by mutual agreement.

9Glossary

Contract
The agreed promise of what a service's interface accepts and returns.
BFF (Backend-for-Frontend)
A backend built to serve one specific frontend.
Data product
A service that publishes a stable, shared interface for many consumers.
Conformance suite
Tests that check a running service actually meets its contract.
Drift
When the two sides' ideas of the contract silently diverge.
Steward
The person/role that owns the contract's policy and approves changes.
Black-box / white-box
Testing only the public contract vs. testing with knowledge of the internals.
